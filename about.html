History of the domain

Chinese names for non-Chinese speakers have long been created through multiple strategies: phonetic transcription, semantic translation, localization, and the preservation of names from other Han-character traditions. These approaches reflect different priorities. Some emphasize sound, some meaning, and others cultural naturalness.

A name recommendation database therefore needs to describe more than the final characters. It must also document the original name, its language, the transformation method, pronunciation, semantic intention, and any cultural factors used during naming.

Intended users

Chinese-language teachersFor explaining pronunciation, meaning, and naming conventions.
Translators and interpretersFor documenting consistent Chinese-name choices.
International studentsFor understanding and comparing recommended names.
Developers and database managersFor building searchable and valid XML records.

Development notes

The schema began as a ten-element application profile. The final design adds inLanguage, fullChineseName, ipa, and hanziRadical to better support multilingual naming records and practical retrieval.

The final XSD also distinguishes between standards used directly and standards used conceptually. Dublin Core Terms is reused through its namespace, while certain person-name concepts informed by Schema.org and FOAF are implemented locally for reliable XML Schema validation.

Theoretical considerations

A recommended Chinese name is treated as a culturally situated identity label rather than a simple text string. Sound, meaning, writing system, cultural association, and user accessibility are modeled as related but separately describable dimensions.
